Introduction

Fast fashion — the rapid, high-volume production of cheap clothing designed to follow the latest trends — has transformed the clothing industry over the past twenty years. Where previous generations bought a few quality garments per season, today's consumers may buy dozens of items per year, wearing many of them only once or twice before discarding them. Students, faced with social pressure to appear up to date and limited by modest budgets, are a primary target market for fast fashion brands.

The consequences for the environment are severe. This page examines the specific environmental harms caused by the fashion industry, how student consumption patterns contribute to those harms, and what practical alternatives are available right now for students in the UK.

Understanding the Problem

The fashion industry is responsible for approximately 10% of global carbon emissions — more than all international aviation and maritime shipping combined. It is also the second-largest consumer of the world's water supply. Producing a single cotton t-shirt requires approximately 2,700 litres of water — enough for one person to drink for two and a half years.

Synthetic fibres like polyester — used in the majority of fast fashion garments — shed microplastics with every wash. These microscopic particles flow through wastewater treatment systems and into rivers, lakes and oceans, where they enter the food chain. A single wash of a polyester garment can release over 700,000 microplastic fibres.

Research consistently finds that young adults aged 18–25 — the core student demographic — are both the heaviest consumers of fast fashion and, increasingly, the group most aware of its environmental impact. This awareness–action gap is driven by several factors specific to student life: budget constraints that make cheap options attractive, social media trends that create constant pressure to dress in new ways, and a lack of convenient alternatives in many university towns.

What Students Can Do

Students do not need to choose between looking good and acting sustainably. A growing ecosystem of affordable, accessible alternatives to fast fashion makes it easier than ever to make responsible clothing choices.

The "Cost Per Wear" Mindset

One of the most effective cognitive shifts a student can make is to think in terms of cost per wear rather than purchase price. A £10 item worn twice costs £5 per wear. A £35 item worn 40 times costs £0.875 per wear. Viewed this way, higher-quality, longer-lasting garments — or well-chosen second-hand items — are almost always the better financial and environmental decision.

Second-Hand & Charity Shops

The UK has one of the most developed second-hand clothing markets in the world. Charity shops, vintage markets, and platforms like Vinted, Depop, and eBay offer high-quality pre-owned garments at prices that frequently undercut fast fashion retailers. Many university cities have thriving charity shop scenes specifically geared towards students.

Clothes Swaps & Borrowing

Clothes swap events — where participants exchange items they no longer wear — are increasingly popular on campuses. Many students' unions organise termly swap events. For one-off events like formal dinners or themed parties, borrowing from flatmates or friends is a zero-cost, zero-waste solution.

Capsule Wardrobes

A capsule wardrobe is a small, carefully chosen collection of versatile garments that can be combined in multiple ways. Rather than following trends, a capsule wardrobe focuses on quality, fit and versatility. Students who adopt this approach typically spend less money overall while producing significantly less textile waste.

GoThrift 26'

GoThrift 26' is an initiative designed to promote sustainable fashion among students. IIT students are encouraged to donate unused clothing, which will then be sold at discounted prices in the EcoLoop Thrift Shop. This initiative reduces textile waste while making affordable clothing accessible to the community.

Action Checklist

Start with one or two actions from this list. Small consistent choices build long-term habits that significantly reduce your clothing footprint.

  • Before buying new clothing, check charity shops, Vinted or Depop first
  • Implement a "cost per wear" mindset — value longevity over cheap price
  • Attend or organise a campus clothes swap event this term
  • Wash synthetic garments in a microplastic-catching laundry bag
  • Repair minor damage (loose buttons, small tears) before discarding items
  • Donate or sell clothing you no longer wear rather than sending it to landfill
  • Unsubscribe from fast fashion brand marketing emails to reduce impulse buying
  • Build a small capsule wardrobe of versatile, mix-and-match items
