Introduction

Food waste is one of the most obvious cases of irresponsible consumption. Every day, huge quantities of edible food are thrown away in homes, restaurants, and supermarkets. This is not only a loss of food but also a loss of the water, energy, money, and labor that went into producing it. In the Sustainable Development Goal 12, responsible consumption and production involve using resources efficiently and minimizing avoidable waste. Food is the best place to start, as it is part of everyday life and something that every student can get involved with.

At the student level, food waste can appear to be a small thing: an uneaten lunch, stale snacks pushed to the back of a cupboard, or buying more than is needed. However, when this behavior is repeated by many people over a period of time, the problem of food waste becomes a serious issue. This page will look at the problem of food waste, why it matters, and how students are contributing to it without realizing it. We will explore what can be done about the issue through everyday actions and explore a student-led initiative called Plate2Purpose which is open to all IIT students!

Understanding the Problem

Food waste is the act of disposing of safe-to-eat food instead of consuming it, donating it to those who might enjoy it, storing it correctly, or utilizing it for better use. Food waste occurs at any stage of the process, from the time we acquire, store, prepare, serve, or even consume food.

For students, food waste is usually caused by poor planning rather than bad intentions. Students may buy food in bulk and not be able to finish it. Others may avoid leftovers in favor of fresh food or may simply forget about them. Edible food is not something that should be discarded like it's garbage. Every item thrown away represents lost value and resources.

The Stats

  1. One-fifth of food produced for human consumption is lost or wasted globally, which is equal to around 1 billion meals a day.
  2. The total cost of food loss and waste for the global economy is estimated at roughly US$1 trillion.
  3. Food loss and waste generates up to 10 per cent of global greenhouse gas emissions.

What Students Can Do

Students do not need large systems or expensive tools to reduce food waste. Simply acting with awareness can make a clear difference.

Plan before buying

Check what is already available before purchasing more. A short list can prevent unnecessary buying.

Take realistic portions

Only buy or serve what can actually be finished. It is better to go back for more than to throw food away.

Store food properly

Dry food should be kept sealed and in suitable conditions. Cooked food should be handled carefully and consumed within a reasonable time.

Use leftovers wisely

Leftovers can often become another meal or snack instead of being ignored.

Share extra food

If there are unopened and safe food items that will not be used, they can be donated or passed on before they expire.

Build awareness

Students can also influence others by speaking about the issue, setting a good example, and joining campus efforts linked to SDG 12, like Plate2Purpose.

Plate2Purpose

Plate2Purpose is a student-led initiative by EcoLoop with the aim of reducing food wastage at IIT. The main objective of this event is to reduce wastage, share smartly, and increase awareness of food wastage on campus. A collection point will be set up on campus where students can donate unopened dry food items (rice, biscuits, noodles, cereal, canned food, milk powder, and other pantry items) with a suitable shelf-life. When possible, campus food stalls or student groups may also contribute untouched surplus food that meets our safety conditions. Students can also volunteer to handle the logistics and flow of the event.

The event will also include activities that will be conducted to spread awareness and encourage responsible consumption and production practices. Students will be able to pledge to Waste Less, participate in the Clean Plate Challenge by buying only what they will eat, share their specific food-saving tips in a community chart, and many more! Incentives will be provided to motivate individuals to participate in the campaign by giving prizes to the top three individuals who contribute and certificates of appreciation to all volunteers who actively participate in the event. Overall, Plate2Purpose 26' helps students understand that reducing food waste is not only about throwing away less, but also about making responsible choices that support both the campus and the wider community.

Action Checklist

Start with one or two of these actions this week. Each one is free, takes under ten minutes to implement, and makes a measurable difference over time.

  • Volunteer for Plate2Purpose
  • Write a weekly meal plan before you shop.
  • Store food correctly (learn fridge vs room temperature for different items).
  • Check what you already have before buying more of the same item
  • Understand the difference between "best before" and "use by" labels
  • Freeze bread, meat and leftovers before they spoil rather than discarding them
  • Use a reusable container to take leftover food home from campus cafeterias
  • Compost food scraps if you have access to a compost bin on campus or at home
